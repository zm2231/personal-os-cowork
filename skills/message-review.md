# Message Review

Comprehensive pre-send review of all communications—grammar, tone, clarity, timing, and appropriateness—before you hit send.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when you want to:
- Review emails before sending
- Double-check important messages
- Avoid communication mistakes
- Get second opinion on sensitive communications
- Build better communication habits

## How It Works

### Level 1: Basic Review Checklist (Sessions 13-21)

I'll help you:
- Check grammar and spelling
- Verify tone and appropriateness
- Ensure clarity and completeness
- Check recipient details
- Identify potential issues

**Example:**
```
User: Review this email before I send it
User: [Paste email draft]
```

### Level 2: Advanced Quality Assurance (Sessions 22-45)

I'll also help you:

I'll also help you:
- Proactively catch communication risks
- Build message templates

- Coach on difficult conversations
- Track long-term communication growth

**Example:**

## Review Categories I Check

### Level 1: Core Review Elements

**Grammar & Mechanics:**
- Spelling and typos
- Grammar errors
- Punctuation
- Capitalization
- Formatting consistency

**Tone & Style:**
- Appropriate for recipient
- Consistent throughout message
- Matches intent and purpose
- Emotional register balanced
- Professional or casual as needed

**Clarity & Completeness:**
- Main message is clear
- All necessary context included
- Call to action is explicit
- No ambiguous statements
- Right amount of detail

**Recipient Accuracy:**
- Correct email address
- Right recipient name
- Appropriate greeting
- Correct subject line
- Relevant attachments included

**Content Quality:**
- No factual errors
- Accurate references
- Truthful and honest
- Appropriate language
- No insensitive content

### Level 2: Advanced Quality Checks

**Timing & Context:**
- Good time to send?
- Urgency appropriate?
- Consider recipient's schedule/time zone
- Holiday or busy period?
- Previous conversation context

**Audience Match:**
- Tone fits recipient relationship
- Complexity appropriate for knowledge level
- Cultural considerations
- Power dynamics respected
- Expectations managed appropriately

**Sensitivity & Diplomacy:**
- Any triggers for recipient?
- Difficult topics handled well?
- Criticism constructive?
- Conflicts managed diplomatically?
- Emotions appropriately expressed

**Risk Assessment:**
- Could this be misunderstood?
- Legal or compliance risks?
- Information security concerns?
- Reputation risks?
- Escalation potential

**Alternatives & Improvements:**
- Could be clearer?
- Could be shorter?
- Better phrasing options
- Additional context needed?
- Different channel better?

## Best Practices

1. **Review everything:** Even quick messages deserve a check
2. **Know your audience:** Recipient context matters
3. **Clarity wins:** Shorter is usually better
4. **Timing matters:** When you send can be as important as what
5. **Learn from mistakes:** Track what went wrong

## Questions I'll Ask

At Level 1:
- "Who is the recipient?"
- "What's the purpose of this message?"
- "Any specific concerns?"
- "What tone do you want?"
- "Anything sensitive to consider?"

At Level 2:
- "When are you planning to send this?"
- "How is your relationship with recipient?"
- "Is there previous context I should know?"
- "Any urgency or deadline?"
- "What outcome do you want?"

At Level 3:
- "Have there been issues with this recipient before?"
- "Is this part of an ongoing conversation?"
- "What communication patterns should I watch for?"
- "Any political or organizational dynamics?"
- "Should we discuss a communication strategy?"

## Examples

### Basic Message Review
```
User: Review this email before I send it

User: "Hi Sarah,

I hope your good. I wanted to discuss the project deadline. Its been moved to next week but their might be some issues. We should meet about this soon.

Thanks,
John"

Assistant: 🔍 Message Review

✅ GOOD:
- Friendly greeting
- Clear purpose (discuss deadline)
- Professional sign-off
- Appropriate for colleague

⚠️ ISSUES FOUND:

**Grammar & Mechanics (3 issues):**
1. "I hope your good" → "I hope you're good" (your vs. you're)
2. "Its been moved" → "It's been moved" (its vs. it's)
3. "their might be" → "there might be" (their vs. there)

**Clarity (2 issues):**
1. "some issues" is vague → specify what issues?
2. "soon" is ambiguous → propose specific time?

**Tone:**
- Tone is appropriate (casual but professional)
- Good for colleague relationship

**Completeness (1 issue):**
- Missing context: What project? Current deadline? New deadline?

**SUGGESTED REVISION:**

"Hi Sarah,

I hope you're doing well.

I wanted to discuss the project deadline. It's been moved to next week, but there might be some scheduling conflicts.

Would you be available to meet tomorrow (Thursday) at 2 PM to discuss?

Thanks,
John"

**Why better:**
- Grammar fixed (your → you're, its → it's, their → there)
- "some scheduling conflicts" more specific than "some issues"
- Proposed specific meeting time (more actionable)
- Added project reference (if you know the project name, add it)

**Rating:** 6/10 → 9/10 after revisions

Ready to send revised version?
```

### Sensitive Message Review
```
User: Review this sensitive email

User: "Hi Mark,

I need to let you know that your application wasn't successful. There were other candidates with more experience.

Better luck next time.

Regards,
Recruiting Team"

Assistant: 🚨 Sensitive Message Review

**RISK LEVEL:** High
**SENSITIVITY:** High
**RELATIONSHIP:** External (candidate)

⚠️ CRITICAL ISSUES:

**Tone Problems:**
1. "Better luck next time" - dismissive, unprofessional
   → Candidate feels undervalued
   → Damages employer brand

2. "There were other candidates" - impersonal
   → No acknowledgment of effort
   → Sounds like generic template

3. No empathy or warmth - too cold
   → Leaves bad impression
   → Could be shared publicly (bad PR)

**Clarity Issues:**
- No explanation of why unsuccessful
- No constructive feedback
- No mention of timeline or next steps

**Missing Elements:**
- No acknowledgment of application
- No specific reason for decision
- No encouragement or positive reinforcement
- No offer for feedback (if candidate wants)
- No professional closing

**RECOMMENDED REVISION:**

"Dear Mark,

Thank you so much for applying for the [Position Name] role and for the time you put into our interview process.

After careful consideration, we've decided to move forward with other candidates whose experience more closely aligns with the specific needs of this role at this time.

I want to acknowledge that this is disappointing news, and I appreciate your interest in joining our team. You have valuable skills and experience, and I encourage you to continue pursuing opportunities that match your strengths.

If you'd like feedback on your application or interview, I'd be happy to schedule a brief call to discuss how you can strengthen future applications.

Wishing you all the best in your next steps.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Title]"

**Why this is better:**
✅ Acknowledges time and effort (shows respect)
✅ Specific reason for decision (transparency)
✅ Validates the disappointment (empathy)
✅ Encourages continued growth (positive)
✅ Offers feedback (constructive)
✅ Professional and warm tone (appropriate)

**ADDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS:**
- Send during business hours (not late at night or weekend)
- Consider phone call for top candidates
- Wait until all interviews complete (don't send too early)

**RISK OF ORIGINAL MESSAGE:** Very High (could damage reputation)
**RISK OF REVISED MESSAGE:** Low (professional, respectful)

**Rating:** 2/10 → 9/10

Would you like me to help with this type of communication more often?
```

### Timing & Context Review
```
User: Is this a good time to send this?

User: "Hi team,

Quick update: I'm pushing the deadline to Friday. We need more time to test everything properly.

Thanks"

[User wants to send at 6:45 PM on Thursday]

Assistant: ⏰ Timing & Context Review

**CURRENT SITUATION:**
- Time: 6:45 PM Thursday
- Recipient: Team (5 people)
- Message: Deadline extension
- Impact: Significant (affects team's plans)

⚠️ TIMING CONCERNS:

**1. Too Late in Day (Thursday)**
- Many people have already left work
- Those still working are wrapping up
- May not see until Friday morning
- Teams may have made plans based on original deadline
- **Risk:** People already allocated Friday for work/commitments

**2. No Notice**
- Last-minute change (Thursday evening for Friday deadline)
- Disrespectful of team's time
- Shows poor planning
- **Risk:** Team frustration, resentment

**3. Impact on Weekend Plans**
- If people planned weekend around Friday completion
- Could affect personal commitments
- **Risk:** Team morale issues

**RECOMMENDATION:**

**Option A: Send NOW with apology (Best for transparency)**
"Hi team,

I know this is late notice, and I apologize for the timing.

I'm pushing the deadline to Friday to ensure we have time for proper testing. This is important for quality, and I don't want to rush.

I realize this may affect your weekend plans. If this creates a major conflict, please let me know and we can discuss alternatives.

I'll be available if you have questions tonight or tomorrow morning."

**Option B: Wait until Friday morning (Better timing)**
Send at 9:00 AM Friday instead. Gives people time to adjust.

**Option C: Send now + follow up Friday (Recommended)**
Send now with apology, then follow up Friday morning to confirm.

**CONTEXT QUESTIONS:**
1. Does anyone have major Friday/Saturday plans this weekend?
2. Is the deadline extension critical or optional?
3. Have you communicated about potential delays earlier this week?
4. What's the consequence of missing the original Friday deadline?

**MY RECOMMENDATION:**
Send now (apology version) + follow up Friday morning. It's late notice, but transparency is better than silence.

**ADDITIONAL SUGGESTIONS:**
- Next time: Communicate deadline risks earlier in the week
- Set expectation early: "Might need to extend to Friday"
- Give team input on deadline changes when possible

**Rating of original timing:** 3/10 (risky)
**Rating of recommended approach:** 8/10 (respectful, transparent)

How would you like to proceed?
```

### Proactive Risk Detection (Level 3)
```
User: "Review this message for risks"

User: "Hi Sam,

I wanted to talk about the project issues we've been having. Some people on the team aren't pulling their weight and it's affecting everyone else.

We should probably talk to management about this. What do you think?

Thanks"

Assistant: ⚠️ Proactive Risk Assessment

**RISK LEVEL: HIGH**
**POTENTIAL IMPACT:** Severe

**RISKS IDENTIFIED:**

**Risk 1: Publicly Criticizing Teammates (HIGH)**
- "Some people on the team aren't pulling their weight"
- This could:
  → Get back to those people
  → Create hostile team environment
  → Damage your reputation
  → Backfire if management asks "who exactly?"
- **Mitigation:** Speak privately to Sam, use general language

**Risk 2: Involving Management Prematurely (HIGH)**
- "We should probably talk to management about this"
- Going over people's heads without:
  → Direct conversation first
  → Specific examples
  → Attempted resolution
- **Mitigation:** Try direct resolution first

**Risk 3: Written Evidence (MEDIUM-HIGH)**
- This email creates permanent record of:
  → Your complaints
  → Your willingness to escalate
  → Potential unprofessionalism
- If forwarded, damages relationships permanently
- **Mitigation:** Have this conversation in person or call

**Risk 4: Vague Accusations (MEDIUM)**
- "Some people" is vague and unfair
- If asked to name names, you're in difficult position
- **Mitigation:** Be specific or don't name at all

**Risk 5: No Proposed Solution (LOW-MEDIUM)**
- Message is all complaint, no action
- Management will ask: "What have you tried to fix this?"
- **Mitigation:** Include steps you've already taken

**STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS:**

**Option 1: DON'T SEND THIS EMAIL (RECOMMENDED)**
This conversation should happen in person or via phone. Email creates permanent record and can be misinterpreted.

**Alternative approach:**
1. Schedule face-to-face meeting with Sam
2. Have open conversation about team dynamics
3. Identify specific behaviors affecting the team
4. Discuss how to address constructively
5. Only escalate to management if direct resolution fails

**Option 2: REWRITE FOR IN-PERSON CONVERSATION SETUP**
"Hi Sam,

I've been observing some team dynamics that I think are affecting our productivity. Could we schedule a quick chat to discuss? I'd value your perspective.

Thanks"

**Better because:**
- Sets up conversation, not email war
- Vague enough to protect privacy
- Collaborative tone
- No accusations in writing

**Option 3: IF YOU MUST SEND EMAIL (NOT RECOMMENDED)**
Rewrite to be constructive and safe:

"Hi Sam,

I've been thinking about how we can improve our team's overall productivity and collaboration. There are some workflow challenges that I think we should address.

Would you be interested in meeting to discuss some ideas for improving team dynamics? I'd like to get your input first before we consider any next steps.

Thanks"

**Better because:**
- Focuses on improvement, not blame
- Collaborative language
- No accusations
- No mention of management escalation

**KEY PRINCIPLE:**
Sensitive team dynamics discussions should never happen via email. The risk of misinterpretation, forwarding, and permanent record is too high.

**NEXT STEPS:**
1. Delete this email draft
2. Schedule in-person meeting with Sam
3. Prepare specific, constructive feedback
4. Focus on behaviors, not people
5. Consider involving manager only as last resort

**Rating of original message:** 2/10 (very risky)
**Rating of recommended approach:** 9/10 (safe, professional)

This is a delicate situation. Want help preparing for the conversation with Sam?
```
